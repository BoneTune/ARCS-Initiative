# The ARCS Commentary v1.0

This document serves as the official companion to the ARCS Manifesto v1.0. It provides context, reasoning, and examples for each principle to help creators and patrons understand the spirit and intent behind the rules.

---

### **I. On Integrity & Communication**

**1. A public release schedule must be defined and adhered to. State clearly from the beginning how often patrons can expect main story updates (e.g., "one chapter every 3 months").**

*   **Commentary:** This rule combats subjectivity, a major source of patron frustration. Vague terms like "regular updates" are meaningless. By forcing a developer to declare a concrete schedule (e.g., a release cycle of X months), this rule establishes a clear, measurable promise. It transforms the relationship from a vague hope into a predictable agreement. If a developer cannot meet their own schedule, they must address it openly (per Rule 2), which prevents a 2-month update cycle from slowly becoming a 12-month cycle without accountability.

**2. If a project is cancelled, paused, or otherwise halted—temporarily or permanently—it must be announced publicly, and all funding channels must be immediately paused or closed.**

*   **Commentary:** This is the ethical backstop against "ghosting" and project abandonment while still collecting funds. It is one of the most critical rules in the manifesto. A temporary pause due to burnout or personal issues is understandable, but patrons must never be charged during periods where the project is not in active development. This rule mandates transparency in failure or hiatus, preserving the creator's long-term reputation and showing fundamental respect for the patrons' investment.

**3. Clear, guided channels for managing patron feedback and offering sustainable rewards must be established and maintained.**

*   **Commentary:** This principle defines communication as a two-way street. It is not enough for the developer to talk; they must also listen. "Guided channels" (like specific polls or feedback threads) allow creators to get useful input without being overwhelmed or having their vision hijacked. The "sustainable rewards" clause is crucial for preventing creator burnout; promising time-consuming rewards (like custom art for many patrons) is a common cause of delays and project failure.

### **II. On Vision & Scope**

**4. Every project must be built on a "Spinal Column": a core document defining its central vision. This vision must be made explicit from the start, and all new content must primarily serve to advance it.**

*   **Commentary:** This rule is the primary defense against "scope creep" and vision drift. The "Spinal Column" is the game's identity—the promise made to early supporters. By requiring all new content to serve this core vision, it prevents the project from becoming diluted with unrelated characters or subplots that delay the main story. Making this vision explicit from the start ensures that everyone—creator and patrons—is on the same page.

**5. "Tag transparency" must be practiced. The project's core content tags and, equally important, a list of "Forbidden Tags" must be clearly declared from the beginning.**

*   **Commentary:** In adult-themed content, managing expectations is paramount. This rule ensures patrons know exactly what they are signing up for, and what they will *never* see. Declaring "Forbidden Tags" is as important as declaring the game's main tags. It prevents future community arguments and disappointment when a creator refuses to add a popular but unwanted feature (e.g., "pregnancy," "rape," etc.). It is an act of radical honesty.

**6. Patrons are valued advisors, not directors. The creator must hold final creative authority to protect the project's core vision.**

*   **Commentary:** This rule establishes a healthy power dynamic. While community feedback is valuable (see Rule 3), a project designed by a committee often ends up bland and directionless. It also protects the creator from being held hostage by a small number of high-paying patrons who may want to steer the project in a direction that betrays the original vision supported by the majority. This rule empowers the creator to say "no" to protect the integrity of the "Spinal Column."

### **III. On Gameplay & Consequence**

**7. The gameplay must be built on player choices that have a meaningful impact, creating distinct and branching narrative paths.**

*   **Commentary:** This is the core requirement that defines an ARCS-compliant project as an interactive experience, not a passive one (like a kinetic novel). "Meaningful impact" means that choices should lead to noticeably different scenes, dialogues, relationships, or outcomes, not just minor flavor text variations. This rule ensures that if a game claims to have choices, those choices matter.

**8. At least one path must lead to a "non-completion" state: a definitive game over where the player fails to reach any of the narrative conclusions.**

*   **Commentary:** This rule distinguishes between a "bad ending" (a narrative conclusion that is tragic or undesirable) and a "game over" (a gameplay failure state). It mandates that the player's actions can lead to a dead end, a premature stop where the story cannot continue. This reinforces the idea that choices have consequences and adds a layer of challenge, respecting the player's ability to fail.

**9. The story must have a developer-intended canon ending, and at least one major alternative canon ending, both of which must be communicated upfront as the project's primary goals.**

*   **Commentary:** This rule ensures narrative purpose and replayability. By defining the main story conclusions from the start, the creator provides a clear goal for both development and player experience. The "main canon" is the resolution the developer considers the definitive story, while the "alternative canon" ensures that the branching paths (mandated by Rule 7) lead to a genuinely different, fully-realized conclusion, not just a minor variation. Communicating these goals upfront manages expectations about the game's ultimate scope.

### **IV. On Production & Delivery**

**10. Funding must not be sought for an idea alone; a creator must present a substantial proof-of-concept (e.g., a polished demo or a complete first chapter). This must be accompanied by realistic promises and a sustainable work pace.**

*   **Commentary:** This is the anti-vaporware rule. It protects patrons from investing in a project that may never exist beyond a few concept images. By requiring a substantial proof-of-concept, the creator demonstrates their technical ability, artistic style, and commitment *before* asking for money. The second clause, regarding realistic promises and a sustainable pace, is included here because the initial project scope and work pace are foundational to delivering on the promise established by the proof-of-concept.

**11. A project must be brought to its promised completion before a remake is considered. Rebooting an unfinished project constitutes a breach of the original commitment.**

*   **Commentary:** This rule addresses a specific and deeply frustrating scenario for patrons who have invested time and money in a story's progress. Seeing a project at 60% completion get rebooted to 0% feels like a betrayal. A developer must finish the race they started. Remakes or engine-swaps are exciting prospects, but they must happen *after* the original promise to the community has been fulfilled.

**12. Side content (specials, DLCs) must not interrupt the promised release schedule of the main story. This content must be developed either after the main story is complete, or in parallel without causing delays to the core updates.**

*   **Commentary:** This rule protects the patrons' primary interest: the advancement of the main story. While holiday specials or side-stories can be fun, they are often seen as a distraction if they are released while the main plot is stalled. This rule ensures that any "extra" content is truly extra and does not come at the expense of the core content that patrons are paying for, as defined by the public release schedule (Rule 1).
